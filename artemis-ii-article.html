Artemis II Is Closing In

NASA’s next crewed Moon mission is being assembled into a real launch moment: four astronauts, a lunar flyby, an official target of April 2026, and one critical caveat from NASA itself, the final launch date is still under review.

Mission Snapshot

The next crewed step in NASA’s lunar return.

Artemis II is not a landing mission. It is the proving flight that has to work first: NASA’s test of the Space Launch System, Orion spacecraft, and the full human deep-space mission chain before astronauts attempt to return to the lunar surface.

Official Target
April 2026

NASA’s Artemis II mission page currently lists the launch target as April 2026.

Flight Profile
Moon Flyby

NASA describes Artemis II as a crewed lunar flyby mission, not a landing attempt.

Crew Count
4

The flight carries four astronauts, including one from the Canadian Space Agency.

Planned Duration
10 Days

NASA says the mission is expected to last about 10 days from launch through return.

When Artemis II launches, it will become NASA’s first crewed mission beyond low-Earth orbit since the Apollo era. That alone makes it historic. But the mission matters for a more practical reason too: it is designed to expose the real behavior of the Orion spacecraft, the Space Launch System rocket, and supporting ground systems with astronauts actually on board.

NASA’s strategy is sequential. Artemis II has to demonstrate the transportation system in deep space before later missions push toward surface operations. In that sense, this flight is both a test and a threshold. If it performs as intended, it becomes the operational bridge between architecture and execution.

Mission Purpose

Validate the full crewed system

NASA is using Artemis II to test launch, deep-space operations, lunar flyby, re-entry, and recovery with a crew aboard.

Program Meaning

De-risk future lunar surface missions

The mission is a prerequisite for more ambitious Artemis flights aimed at returning astronauts to the Moon.

Historical Weight

First crewed deep-space NASA mission in decades

A successful flight would mark NASA’s return to human missions beyond low-Earth orbit after more than 50 years.

Launch Reality

The target is real. The exact date is not locked.

NASA’s official message is precise: Artemis II is targeted for April 2026, but the public launch listing still marks timing as under review. That distinction matters because the mission is still passing through readiness gates.

Crew

Four astronauts, one system test, one lunar swing around the far side.

NASA lists Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Jeremy Hansen as the Artemis II crew. Their mission is not to land, but to prove that the entire transportation stack can carry humans safely around the Moon and back.
